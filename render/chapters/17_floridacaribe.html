February 24, 2016

Interview on the Florida Caribe Show

Paola Baez & Gerina Gjergji, Florida Caribe Show; Ryan Catalani, Tamera Marko, Ph.D.
This is a transcript of a live radio interview with the Florida Caribe Show, a program on the community radio station WSLR in Sarasota, Florida.

Gerina Gjergji: So now we have an interview with Ryan Catalani and Tamera Marko, who are the co-founders of a nonprofit organization known as Mobility Movilidad and are going to talk to us about one of their projects called Proyecto Carrito. Proyecto Carrito focuses on the story of undergraduates and janitors that have begun evolving identities as writers and have dreams for more inclusive and global 21st-century education in a translingual class at Emerson College.

Paola Baez: Welcome to the show, Ryan and Tamera. How are you guys?

Tamera Marko: Hi.

Ryan Catalani: Good, how are you?

Baez: So we'll start off with a very basic question: What is Proyecto Carrito?

Marko: So Proyecto Carrito is a collective, and as you mentioned, it's workers, undergraduate students, graduate students, administrators and professors at Emerson College in Boston, and we are all writers. And what we're trying to do is identify where we have borders inside our own institution regarding access to 21st-century inclusive education. So that means everything from classes to library access to issues regarding documentation and status within the institution, so what kind of job you do.

And we believe that, since we're all in the same educational institution, we should have access to all the same resources. And how can you do that even with our respective job differences— say one cleans the bathroom primarily, and the other one is teaching in a classroom. And so that's what we do and we come around the table. We've been together for almost five years now. And we discuss where our borders and blockages are, and where our dreams are, and then we figure out how to gain access with each other. And we do so by developing relationships, first with each other, and then with the rest of the campus.

Baez: That's really interesting. I love when you mention it doesn't matter what work we do—if someone's teaching in a classroom, someone's cleaning the bathroom, they still have the right to equal amount of educational opportunity. That's very important. And I want to ask, what prompted this type of program to be started—what was the spark for this?

Marko: So one of the ways that this started was—this was about five years ago, the members of the administration here met with the facilities staff—maintenance workers staff—and asked them, “If you could have one thing, right now, to make your quality of life better at work at Emerson College, what would be? What would you want?” And they all asked for English class. So they wanted an English language learning class. And so then they called me, or one of the administrators called me, and said, “Well, we see that you speak Spanish, and you teach English, could you maybe consider doing a class for a semester?”

And I knew right away that there is no way that in one hour a week, people are going to learn English, but I did know—what a privilege to be able to get to know members of the maintenance workers staff, and to work with people who are experts at our institution in immigration and resilience and building a new life, and also they're fundamental to the quality of our education here, in the sense that they care for the very infrastructure within which we live and work.

So I jumped on it. And then we met together and I said, “Do you want me as your teacher?” I don't really think we can learn English in one hour a week, but we can identify other dreams that you have, and ways in which English is important to those, and also just language in general and writing in Spanish, so that's what we did.

And to my surprise—to all of our surprise I think—we're still together five years later. And now we're like a familia—we're a family—we eat together, party together, we know about each other's children. When the abuelito or a grandmother dies, we are cheering for each other. When there's a blizzard, we're texting each other, “Are you okay?”, which is interesting, because as a professor, I'm home, and I have a snow day, and I know they're out shoveling the snow. So I'm worried about them, so I'm writing to make sure they're okay. So, Ryan, you want to say anything?

Catalani: Yeah, so we come together every week, once a week, and when someone new comes to the class, we first introduce ourselves and say why we're here. And what's interesting about that is, if you come to it, you'll see that what everyone says why they come is that it's the best and most important part of their week—that this one hour we spend together is something so different from what they experience at Emerson, and that's true for all participants in the class, that it's sort of an oasis. And not that it should be. We think that more places should be like this at Emerson and other educational institutions, but for now, it's a very special community that we created.

Baez: And what type of importance do you think a program like this has? I mean, you already kind of spoke on that and what it can achieve, and what it can do for a community, but what do you think it has on the development of richer, more culturally diverse communities—not only Emerson, but how do you think this could be applied to other communities? You think it's a transferable idea? What do you think about that, about the idea of moving this around?

Marko: Yeah, I think that's a two-part question, I think, and really important. It's actually the core of our mission, and why we're still together as a collective meeting once a week, because we could just stay as a familia, and be friends, and that in itself would be meaningful.

So we met on Wednesday last week, when we knew we were coming to talk on your show, and we wanted them to be here—the people who work, the workers—but they couldn't be here at this time because they're working. But we actually interviewed them and asked them what they would say, so we've sent you some of those sound bites—

Baez: —yeah, we have a clip ready to play at the end—

Marko: —we'll hear them later—

Baez: —yeah—

Marko: —but one of the things that they say when—because I used to feel like, are we really doing anything? We're not unionizing, we're not protesting in the way that a protest generally looks like. We are in a conference room for one hour a week, singing and writing, and it is very meaningful for us, but what are we really doing? How are we changing the campus?

At first that's what I thought, in the first year or two—we were just building relationships. And one of the things they said was, you know if we could change anything about our education or about our experience at an educational facility, we would change three things: One, we would want to learn—we want everyone to learn more about context of why people immigrate, and thus why we have immigrated. Two, to be more united among Latinas and Latinos. So for example, if a Latino or Latin American worker and student encounter each other in an elevator, they should say “Buenos días,” say “Good morning,” acknowledge each other, so that the workers aren't fantasmas, ghosts.

And the third thing they said—and this is so important to them and still is—they said please say “Good morning” to us. Please acknowledge that we exist. And so when they talk about—with other people, and we do talk at conferences and other places, we have publications, we've made documentary videos, which now circulating in classrooms throughout Emerson but also nationwide, K-20 schools, by the way—so kindergarten through university and college level—but what they say is that being seen at their institution where they've been working for 10 years, 26 years, 30 years, is one of the most important changes for them. And it means a lot, it's dignity.

The other thing we have accomplished is that the immigrant workers are now teachers. Not formally—they don't get credit for it, and they don't get paid for it, which I would really like to see, them being compensated for it. But they invited undergraduates into our once-a-week class. And the undergraduates come in and you can see it—they transform. Many times they're shaken to the core. They cry, because they realize that person that they've been seen in their dorm room or dorm, cleaning the toilet every week or every day, is now sitting there, teaching them something, and listening to them, or maybe just caring for them, and they see them. And so then they face that—the beauty of it, but also the contradiction of: Wait a minute, why is it that some people are at this institution cleaning toilets, and I'm here getting a diploma? Why? And so they're facing something they haven't really thought of before.

And then the last thing I would say that we've accomplished, at least at Emerson College, are some very concrete changes. So human resources here, now, is translating all of their documents into Spanish. When they hold an event, they have translators. They have put members of the maintenance worker stuff on committees, so when they have meetings to make changes about all kinds of things, like policies and infrastructure, the maintenance worker staff is now represented, instead of just faculty and students. They have a library cards. They have access to the computer. And they've been an integral part of a committee at which Emerson—now Emerson College officially accepts people regardless of their documentation. So we have students who apply here, are recruited, and can even receive some funding, regardless of their documentation status. So they—thee workers weigh in on that too.

And then in terms of, is this idea replicable, is it a model? It seems like other people think so, because we've been getting phone calls from throughout Boston and across the United States, and also in other countries too. Interestingly enough, Colombia's really interested in what we do, Mexico, Brazil—they find us online or we present at a conference.

So what we've begun doing is working in consultation with different institutions and schools and some workplaces. And we meet with the people who clean the building. They usually are immigrants, and often they're from Latin America, but sometimes they're from all over the world. And we start with the workers and we go from there. And we ask: If you could make your workplace more inclusive, right now, what would it be? And then we start working on that. And we identify people who are interested and who care about these issues inside the institution. And we work with elders, and youth, and—because everyone has different kinds of power at the same institution.

And that's how we begin to work with them to create change. And we do that through just sitting around the table and talking. We do writing projects together, we do storytelling, meals, and it's a very slow, painstaking process, because it's about trust, and it's about risk: The people whose stories have the most to teach us risk the most in telling us, and they often benefit the least. So we have to work building trust with each other, and it has to be authentic, because if it's not, everything falls apart in five seconds.

Baez: Yeah, I think it's very interesting what you said about—it simply starts by sitting around a conference table, and just talking, and getting to know each other, and trust. And I feel like that's something that's really missing in the world in general—just understanding the person that's sitting across from you. You may have come from very different circumstances, and just understanding them, is the first step.

Gjergji: And the first step could also be just saying “Hi” to people who work in our environments and creating a communication and discourse about identity and our experiences. It's a really great project. So can you tell us a bit about the Mobility Movilidad, and where does Proyecto Carrito fit into it?

Catalani: Sure, so Mobility Movilidad is a nonprofit that Tam and I and another director, Jota Samper, started last year. And it sort of grew out of the work that we've been doing together for the last four years. So Proyecto Carrito is one of our three main initiatives that we have. And they're all about this idea that Tam was talking about, about bringing people to the table who don't often come together, and choreographing conversations with diverse audiences about stories that are not often told.

So the other projects that we have: One is a documentary archive in Medellín, Columbia, where we work with women and families who were forced to flee their homes, usually in rural areas, due to violence or poverty, and rebuild their homes in the city of Medellín—and they've been doing that for the last 50 years. And so this archive is a historical record of how the City of Medellín was really built, by the people who really built them, with their own hands. And so we do that every summer—we work with more storytellers to create more documentaries.

And then another—the other project that we have is that we work with young, emerging Colombian artists to help them come travel with their art—cross borders with their art and their stories. So we help them come to the US to do exhibits of their work, and also talk with audiences about their work, which is often about 21st-century— some of our most pressing sustainability and human rights issues. And they talk about these very global issues through very local perspective. That really makes it powerful for audiences across the country that see their work and hear them talk about it.

Marko: So they work on water rights, women's rights, youth culture, hip-hop art, different things that are being produced. And oftentimes it’s not just about humanitarian and environmental crises—it's about solutions to it. And it's often young people and women, but not always. And we work with people of any age and from any neighborhood and any educational background. And what we do is, we work with them to figure out what is it they want to say, who do they want to say it to, why. And then we work with them to do all the kinds of multimedia writing necessary for the storyteller and story to cross borders together. That's the hardest part, especially coming from a place like Columbia —getting a travel visa and so forth.

So we choreograph all of that kind of transnational communication with them, personally, online. We will prepare them to go to the US Embassy, all kinds of things like that, to ask for their visa. Then we choreograph conversations here in the United States. So our last tour, for example, with nine young, emerging artists from Universidad Nacional de Colombia en Medellín, which is a university there, public university, we choreograph conversations with them so they can speak with audiences in five different cities in Hawaii, in Los Angeles—so in Honolulu, Los Angeles, Boston, Durham, and Medellín, in Colombia. And in each of those places, with each of those audiences, it's a different way of presenting information.

It's also—we ask the audience to do some work, too. So we expect it to be a little bit challenging for the audiences. Not to challenge them on purpose—but just that they're coming to the table to have a real conversation. It's a little bit different than just coming to museum and observing something beautiful, which is also important—I think that has its place—but what we do is a little bit different. We treat the hour that we have together—or the five hours, or whatever it is—with our audiences as sacred moments that make consciousness, social change, and relationship-building possible.

And then what we try to do with Mobility Movilidad that is we help the storyteller craft their story in a way—like making a documentary, maybe it's a photo essay, they give a speech, we also help them work with the media, all kinds of things—and then we back away. So Ryan and I and Jota, the directors of the nonprofit, we back away and then let the people who need to come together and have conversations to stop pollution in a well, or talk about ways that people can have access to education—possible. And we back away. And then two people, or the 10 people, or the hundred people, who need to come together at the table, keep coming together, and can get done what they want to get done.

Baez: Now that you've mentioned your other projects, and the impact that they have on the people you visit in the communities—just a little bit more about the type of reception and impact you think the caravans, and also including this new—these other projects, have made on the communities that you have visited. What type of impact do you think you have made with other people who are—don't know about you, but then you show up and talk to them, and you express this, what do you think goes through their minds?

Marko: Well, that's a really good question, and that's something—I obsess about this, because I want to make sure that what we're doing—it matters to people in ways that are that are meaningful to them.

And so we were at University of California, Irvine a couple months ago. And I don't know if you heard—remember this, but there was a big achievement in all of the University of California system, of unionization of maintenance workers. And UC Irvine was the place of one of the deepest contentions of all the UC system, just because many of the workers had been working under duress. They didn't have very much of any vacation time, and so forth. So they were working in conditions that were very, very difficult, and have been doing so for more than 20 years. Many of them raise children, for example, without health insurance, ever, things like that.

And so we went to UC Irvine at the invitation of a faculty member who wanted to begin a writing project, found us online, invited to come. So we flew out to California from Boston, and met with them. And we met with about six—mostly women—who were maintenance workers. And they had done already great achievements—they'd organized, they'd become unionized, they had different pay, they have vacation—really big things, and also gotten a lot of media attention, and many of them were already grandmothers by then. And they were—they could've just said, “We're done. We've achieved our thing, we're done.”

We showed them our video, which you can find on our website, mobilitymovilidad.org, and we showed them that same video of the maintenance workers here at Emerson, talking about what we do, presenting at a national writing studies conference, talking with undergraduate professors and administrators. And the women were transfixed, because they said, “You know, we unionized, we've done all these things, but we're still ghosts on campus—it didn't change very much our daily interactions with people, in terms of the way that we experience our daily life at work. We need that to change. That's what we have to do next. And the only way that that can happen is if we come together face-to-face, and we know each other.” And so that's what they wanted to do.

And then the other element of that is that because Proyecto Carrito for us is actually a car. “Carrito,” as you know, means “little car.” It also means “my darling, beloved car” because of the “-ito” there, which is the diminutive in Spanish. And so we actually publish our work on a van, roof to rim, our stories. There are giant pictures of us, and we drive, when we go places to present, we drive it and we're in it and then we pile out of it. We're in a parking lot, so people see our stories on the side of the van, or the back of the van, or the trunk of the van, or whatever, then they see us come out. And there are pictures of us on the van, then we come out of the van, and we're standing next to the van. So we have all these conversations with people in parking lots, at toll booths, outside of restaurants, in addition to the more formal conference presentations that we give. And then finally this summer, we're going to do a caravan, and Ryan can talk about that.

Catalani: So basically, so we want to make more Proyecto Carritos with other institutions, or other groups that are interested in this work of making our education system more inclusive, and global, and humane, and they should be immigrant-led efforts also. And so we're going to drive this summer, in July, from Boston to the San Diego-Tijuana border, with other cars and other groups that are going to make cars or vans or other vehicles, wrap them with their stories, and their dreams and practices for a more inclusive education system.

And we'll drive together, through a very carefully chosen route, across the country, and make some strategic stops with audiences that we've developed, that we've prepared to talk with us. And then we'll end on the West Coast, and sort of all come together. And so the trip will take probably about two weeks. And groups that are interested can still contact us on the website, and talk to us about how they can be part of the Caravan this summer.

Baez: And the website will be—what's the website name, for people who want to?

Catalani: Yeah, the website—for the Caravan, you can just go to proyectocarrito.org.

Baez: OK, well, thank you so much, Ryan and Tamera, for talking with us this morning. We have a—last week, Ryan called me to let me know that they were in class on Wednesday, and if I could send a question over to get some recordings of the actual students in the class—of the people who participate in the class—their actual answers. And so the question I sent was, “What would you do to change the world?” So now we're going to play a clip of some of those answers. Thank you so much, Ryan and Tamera.

Gjergji: Thank you so much, muchisimas gracias.

And the undergraduates come in and you can see it—they transform. Many times they're shaken to the core. They cry, because they realize that person that they've been seen in their dorm room or dorm, cleaning the toilet every week or every day, is now sitting there, teaching them something, and listening to them, or maybe just caring for them, and they see them. And so then they face that—the beauty of it, but also the contradiction of: Wait a minute, why is it that some people are at this institution cleaning toilets, and I'm here getting a diploma? Why? And so they're facing something they haven't really thought of before.

And then the last thing I would say that we've accomplished, at least at Emerson College, are some very concrete changes. So human resources here, now, is translating all of their documents into Spanish. When they hold an event, they have translators. They have put members of the maintenance worker stuff on committees, so when they have meetings to make changes about all kinds of things, like policies and infrastructure, the maintenance worker staff is now represented, instead of just faculty and students. They have a library cards. They have access to the computer. And they've been an integral part of a committee at which Emerson—now Emerson College officially accepts people regardless of their documentation. So we have students who apply here, are recruited, and can even receive some funding, regardless of their documentation status. So they—thee workers weigh in on that too.

And then in terms of, is this idea replicable, is it a model? It seems like other people think so, because we've been getting phone calls from throughout Boston and across the United States, and also in other countries too. Interestingly enough, Colombia's really interested in what we do, Mexico, Brazil—they find us online or we present at a conference.

So what we've begun doing is working in consultation with different institutions and schools and some workplaces. And we meet with the people who clean the building. They usually are immigrants, and often they're from Latin America, but sometimes they're from all over the world. And we start with the workers and we go from there. And we ask: If you could make your workplace more inclusive, right now, what would it be? And then we start working on that. And we identify people who are interested and who care about these issues inside the institution. And we work with elders, and youth, and—because everyone has different kinds of power at the same institution.

And that's how we begin to work with them to create change. And we do that through just sitting around the table and talking. We do writing projects together, we do storytelling, meals, and it's a very slow, painstaking process, because it's about trust, and it's about risk: The people whose stories have the most to teach us risk the most in telling us, and they often benefit the least. So we have to work building trust with each other, and it has to be authentic, because if it's not, everything falls apart in five seconds.

Baez: Yeah, I think it's very interesting what you said about—it simply starts by sitting around a conference table, and just talking, and getting to know each other, and trust. And I feel like that's something that's really missing in the world in general—just understanding the person that's sitting across from you. You may have come from very different circumstances, and just understanding them, is the first step.

Gjergji: And the first step could also be just saying “Hi” to people who work in our environments and creating a communication and discourse about identity and our experiences. It's a really great project. So can you tell us a bit about the Mobility Movilidad, and where does Proyecto Carrito fit into it?

Catalani: Sure, so Mobility Movilidad is a nonprofit that Tam and I and another director, Jota Samper, started last year. And it sort of grew out of the work that we've been doing together for the last four years. So Proyecto Carrito is one of our three main initiatives that we have. And they're all about this idea that Tam was talking about, about bringing people to the table who don't often come together, and choreographing conversations with diverse audiences about stories that are not often told.

So the other projects that we have: One is a documentary archive in Medellín, Columbia, where we work with women and families who were forced to flee their homes, usually in rural areas, due to violence or poverty, and rebuild their homes in the city of Medellín—and they've been doing that for the last 50 years. And so this archive is a historical record of how the City of Medellín was really built, by the people who really built them, with their own hands. And so we do that every summer—we work with more storytellers to create more documentaries.

And then another—the other project that we have is that we work with young, emerging Colombian artists to help them come travel with their art—cross borders with their art and their stories. So we help them come to the US to do exhibits of their work, and also talk with audiences about their work, which is often about 21st-century— some of our most pressing sustainability and human rights issues. And they talk about these very global issues through very local perspective. That really makes it powerful for audiences across the country that see their work and hear them talk about it.

Marko: So they work on water rights, women's rights, youth culture, hip-hop art, different things that are being produced. And oftentimes it’s not just about humanitarian and environmental crises—it's about solutions to it. And it's often young people and women, but not always. And we work with people of any age and from any neighborhood and any educational background. And what we do is, we work with them to figure out what is it they want to say, who do they want to say it to, why. And then we work with them to do all the kinds of multimedia writing necessary for the storyteller and story to cross borders together. That's the hardest part, especially coming from a place like Columbia —getting a travel visa and so forth.

So we choreograph all of that kind of transnational communication with them, personally, online. We will prepare them to go to the US Embassy, all kinds of things like that, to ask for their visa. Then we choreograph conversations here in the United States. So our last tour, for example, with nine young, emerging artists from Universidad Nacional de Colombia en Medellín, which is a university there, public university, we choreograph conversations with them so they can speak with audiences in five different cities in Hawaii, in Los Angeles—so in Honolulu, Los Angeles, Boston, Durham, and Medellín, in Colombia. And in each of those places, with each of those audiences, it's a different way of presenting information.

It's also—we ask the audience to do some work, too. So we expect it to be a little bit challenging for the audiences. Not to challenge them on purpose—but just that they're coming to the table to have a real conversation. It's a little bit different than just coming to museum and observing something beautiful, which is also important—I think that has its place—but what we do is a little bit different. We treat the hour that we have together—or the five hours, or whatever it is—with our audiences as sacred moments that make consciousness, social change, and relationship-building possible.

And then what we try to do with Mobility Movilidad that is we help the storyteller craft their story in a way—like making a documentary, maybe it's a photo essay, they give a speech, we also help them work with the media, all kinds of things—and then we back away. So Ryan and I and Jota, the directors of the nonprofit, we back away and then let the people who need to come together and have conversations to stop pollution in a well, or talk about ways that people can have access to education—possible. And we back away. And then two people, or the 10 people, or the hundred people, who need to come together at the table, keep coming together, and can get done what they want to get done.

Baez: Now that you've mentioned your other projects, and the impact that they have on the people you visit in the communities—just a little bit more about the type of reception and impact you think the caravans, and also including this new—these other projects, have made on the communities that you have visited. What type of impact do you think you have made with other people who are—don't know about you, but then you show up and talk to them, and you express this, what do you think goes through their minds?

Marko: Well, that's a really good question, and that's something—I obsess about this, because I want to make sure that what we're doing—it matters to people in ways that are that are meaningful to them.

And so we were at University of California, Irvine a couple months ago. And I don't know if you heard—remember this, but there was a big achievement in all of the University of California system, of unionization of maintenance workers. And UC Irvine was the place of one of the deepest contentions of all the UC system, just because many of the workers had been working under duress. They didn't have very much of any vacation time, and so forth. So they were working in conditions that were very, very difficult, and have been doing so for more than 20 years. Many of them raise children, for example, without health insurance, ever, things like that.

And so we went to UC Irvine at the invitation of a faculty member who wanted to begin a writing project, found us online, invited to come. So we flew out to California from Boston, and met with them. And we met with about six—mostly women—who were maintenance workers. And they had done already great achievements—they'd organized, they'd become unionized, they had different pay, they have vacation—really big things, and also gotten a lot of media attention, and many of them were already grandmothers by then. And they were—they could've just said, “We're done. We've achieved our thing, we're done.”

We showed them our video, which you can find on our website, mobilitymovilidad.org, and we showed them that same video of the maintenance workers here at Emerson, talking about what we do, presenting at a national writing studies conference, talking with undergraduate professors and administrators. And the women were transfixed, because they said, “You know, we unionized, we've done all these things, but we're still ghosts on campus—it didn't change very much our daily interactions with people, in terms of the way that we experience our daily life at work. We need that to change. That's what we have to do next. And the only way that that can happen is if we come together face-to-face, and we know each other.” And so that's what they wanted to do.

And then the other element of that is that because Proyecto Carrito for us is actually a car. “Carrito,” as you know, means “little car.” It also means “my darling, beloved car” because of the “-ito” there, which is the diminutive in Spanish. And so we actually publish our work on a van, roof to rim, our stories. There are giant pictures of us, and we drive, when we go places to present, we drive it and we're in it and then we pile out of it. We're in a parking lot, so people see our stories on the side of the van, or the back of the van, or the trunk of the van, or whatever, then they see us come out. And there are pictures of us on the van, then we come out of the van, and we're standing next to the van. So we have all these conversations with people in parking lots, at toll booths, outside of restaurants, in addition to the more formal conference presentations that we give. And then finally this summer, we're going to do a caravan, and Ryan can talk about that.

Catalani: So basically, so we want to make more Proyecto Carritos with other institutions, or other groups that are interested in this work of making our education system more inclusive, and global, and humane, and they should be immigrant-led efforts also. And so we're going to drive this summer, in July, from Boston to the San Diego-Tijuana border, with other cars and other groups that are going to make cars or vans or other vehicles, wrap them with their stories, and their dreams and practices for a more inclusive education system.

And we'll drive together, through a very carefully chosen route, across the country, and make some strategic stops with audiences that we've developed, that we've prepared to talk with us. And then we'll end on the West Coast, and sort of all come together. And so the trip will take probably about two weeks. And groups that are interested can still contact us on the website, and talk to us about how they can be part of the Caravan this summer.

Baez: And the website will be—what's the website name, for people who want to?

Catalani: Yeah, the website—for the Caravan, you can just go to proyectocarrito.org.

Entrevista con Florida Caribe Show

Paola Baez & Gerina Gjergji, Florida Caribe Show; Ryan Catalani, Tamera Marko, Ph.D.
Esta es una transcripción de una entrevista de radio en vivo con el Florida Caribe Show, un programa de la emisora WSLR en Sarasota, Florida.

Gerina Gjergji: Ahora tenemos una entrevista con Ryan Catalani y Tamera Marko, quienes son los co-fundadores de una organización sin ánimo de lucro que se llama Mobility Movilidad, y nos van a hablar acerca de uno de sus proyectos llamado Proyecto Carrito. Proyecto Carrito se enfoca en la historia de estudiantes de pregrado y trabajadores de aseo que han empezado a evolucionar sus identidades como escritores y tienen sueños para una educación más inclusiva y global del siglo 21 en una clase translingüe en Emerson College.

Paola Baez: Bienvenidos al programa, Ryan y Tamera. ¿Cómo están?

Tamera Marko: Hola.

Ryan Catalani: Bien, ¿cómo están?

Baez: Empezamos con una pregunta muy básica: ¿Qué es Proyecto Carrito?

Marko: Proyecto Carrito es un colectivo, y como mencionaste, está formado por trabajadores, estudiantes de pregrado, estudiantes de posgrado, administradores y profesores en Emerson College en Boston, y todos somos escritores. Y lo que estamos intentando a hacer es identificar dónde tenemos fronteras dentro de nuestra institución con respecto a acceso a la educación inclusiva del siglo 21. Esto significa todo desde las clases hasta el acceso a la biblioteca hasta los asuntos con respecto a la documentación y estatus dentro de la institución, o sea la clase de trabajo que uno hace.

Y creemos que, porque todos estamos en la misma institución educativa, deberíamos tener acceso a todos los mismos recursos. Y cómo puede uno hacer esto aún considerando las diferencias de nuestros respectivos trabajos—digamos que uno limpia el baño primariamente, y el otro está enseñando en una aula. Eso es lo que hacemos y venimos todos a la mesa. Hemos estado juntos por casi cinco años ahora. Y discutimos dónde están nuestras fronteras y obstrucciones, y dónde están nuestros sueños, y luego resolvemos como ganar acesso los unos con los otros. Y lo hacemos a través de desarrollar relaciones, primero dentro del grupo, y después con el resto del campus.

Baez: Eso es muy interesante. Me encanta cuando mencionas que no importa el trabajo que hacemos—si alguien está enseñando en una clase, alguien está limpiando el baño, todavía tiene el derecho a una cantidad igual de oportunidad educativa. Eso es muy importante. Y quiero preguntar, ¿qué fue lo que dió lugar a esta clase de programa—qué fue la chispa de esto?

Marko: Una de las maneras en que esto empezó era—hace como cinco años, los miembros de la administración acá se reunieron con el personal del complejo—el personal de los trabajadores de aseo—y les preguntó, “¿Si pudieran tener una cosa, ahora mismo, para mejorar su calidad de vida acá en el trabajo en Emerson College, qué sería? ¿Qué querrían?” Y todos pidieron una clase de inglés. Entonces querían una clase de aprendizaje de inglés. Y entonces me llamaron, o uno de los administradores me llamó, y dijo, “Pues, vimos que hablas español, y enseñas inglés, ¿puedes tal vez pensar en hacer una clase por un semestre?”

Y sabía inmediatamente que no sería posible que, en una hora por semana, la gente aprendiera inglés, pero sí sabía—qué privilegio, poder conocer a miembros del personal de aseo, y trabajar con personas que son expertos en nuestra institución acerca de inmigración y resiliencia y construir una vida nueva, y también son fundamentales a la calidad de nuestra educación aquí, en el sentido que cuidan de la misma infraestructura, dentro de la cual que vivimos y trabajamos.

Entonces lo aproveché. Y luego nos reunimos juntos y dije, “¿Quieren que sea su profesora? No creo que podemos aprender inglés en una hora por semana, pero sí podemos identificar otros sueños que tienen, y las maneras en que el inglés es importante para aquellos, y también idiomas en general y la escritura en español,” así que eso es lo que hicimos.

Y para mi sorpresa—para toda nuestra sorpresa, creo—todavía estamos juntos, cinco años luego. Y ahora somos como una familia—comemos juntos, nos divertimos juntos, sabemos de todos nuestros hijos. Cuando el abuelito o una abuela se muere, nos estamos reconfortando unos a otros. Cuando hay una tormenta de nieve, nos estamos mandando textos, “¿Estás bien?”, que es interesante, porque como profesora, estoy en mi hogar, y no tengo que trabajar, y sé que están cavando la nieve. Ryan, ¿quieres decir algo?

Catalani: Sí, nos reunimos cada semana, una vez a la semana, y cuando alguien nuevo viene a la clase, primero nos presentamos y decimos por qué estamos aquí. Y lo interesante de esto es, si uno viene a ella, vería que lo que cada persona dice por qué viene es porque es la parte mejor y más importante de su semana—que esta una hora que compartimos es algo tan distinto de lo que experimentan en Emerson, y eso es cierto para todos los participantes en la clase, que es como un oasis. Y no es que debería ser esto. Creemos que más lugares deberían ser más así en Emerson y otras instituciones educativas, pero por ahora, es una comunidad muy especial que creamos.

Baez: ¿Y qué clase de importancia consideran que un programa así tiene? Ya hablaron un poco acerca de esto y lo que puede lograr, y lo que puede hacer para una comunidad, pero ¿qué efecto piensas que tiene en el desarrollo de comunidades más ricos, más diversas culturalmente? No sólo Emerson, pero ¿cómo piensan que se puede aplicar esto a otras comunidades? ¿Piensan que es una idea transferible? ¿Qué piensan de eso, acerca de la idea de extender esto?

Marko: Sí, creo que esta pregunta tiene dos partes, creo, y es muy importante. En realidad es la fundación de nuestra misión, y por qué todavía estamos juntos como un colectivo, reuniéndonos una vez por semana, porque simplemente pudieramos quedarnos como familia, y ser amigos, y esto en sí sería significativo.

Nos reunimos el miércoles pasado, cuando sabíamos que vendremos a hablar en su programa, y queríamos que estén—los que trabajan, los trabajadores—pero no pudieron estar aquí en esta hora porque están trabajando. Pero en realidad los entrevistamos y les preguntamos lo que dirían, entonces les hemos enviado unos de estos fragmentos—

Baez: —sí, tenemos un fragmento listo para reproducir al fin—

Marko: —los escucharemos luego—

Baez: —sí—

Marko: —pero una de las cosas que dicen cuando—porque antes me sentía como, ¿realmente estamos haciendo algo? No estamos sindicalizando, no estamos protestando en la forma que una protesta generalmente aparece. Estamos en una sala de conferencia por una hora cada semana, cantando y escribiendo, y es muy significativo para nosotros, pero ¿realmente qué estamos haciendo? ¿Cómo estamos cambiando el campus?

En el principio esto es lo que pensaba, en el primer año o dos años—sólo estábamos construyendo relaciones. Y una de las cosas que dijeron fue, si pudiéramos cambiar algo acerca de nuestra educación o nuestra experiencia en un centro educativo, cambiaríamos tres cosas: Uno, querríamos aprender—queremos que todos aprendan más acerca del contexto de por qué la gente migra, y así por qué hemos migrado. Dos, ser más unidos entre Latinas y Latinos. Por ejemplo, si un trabajador y un estudiante latino o latinoamericano se encuentran en un ascensor, deberían decir “Buenos días,” reconocerse, para que los trabajadores no sean fantasmas.

Y la tercera cosa que dijeron—y esto les importaba tanto y les importa todavía—dijeron por favor nos digan “Buenos días.” Por favor reconozcan que existimos. Y cuando hablan sobre—con otra gente, y sí hablamos en conferencias y otros lugares, tenemos publicaciones, hemos hecho videos documentales, que están circulando en aulas en todo Emerson y a escala nacional, escuelas K-20, por cierto—desde jardín hasta universidad—pero lo que dicen es que el ser visto en su institución donde han trabajado por 10 años, 26 años, 30 años, es uno de los cambios más importantes para ellos. Y les significa mucho, es dignidad.

La otra cosa que hemos logrado es que los trabajadores inmigrantes ahora son profesores. No formalmente—no reciben crédito, y no le pagan, que me gustaría mucho ver, que son compensados. Pero invitaron a estudiantes de pregrado a nuestra clase semanal. Y los estudiantes vienen y uno lo puede ver—se transforman. Muchas veces están afectados profundamente. Lloran, porque les dan cuenta de que la persona que han visto en su cuarto del dormitorio, o su dormitorio, limpiando el baño cada semana o cada día, ya está sentado allí, enseñándoles algo, y escuchándolos, o quizás simplemente cuidándoles, y los ven. Y cuando enfrentan eso—lo bonito, pero también la contradicción de: Espérate, ¿por qué unas personas en esta institución están limpiando baños, y yo estoy aquí para recibir una diploma? ¿Por qué? Y están enfrentando algo que realmente no habían pensando antes.

Y la última cosa que diría que hemos logrado, al menos en Emerson College, son unos cambios muy concretos. Recursos humanos aquí, ahora, está traduciendo todos sus documentos en español. Cuando tienen un evento, llevan traductores. Les han involucrado a miembros del personal de aseo en comités, así cuando tienen reuniones para cambiar varias cosas, como políticas e infraestructura, el personal de aseo ya está representado, en vez de sólo profesores y estudiantes. Tienen tarjetas de biblioteca. Tienen acceso al computador. Y han sido una parte integral de un comité en que Emerson—ahora Emerson College oficialmente admite a personas sin tener en cuenta su documentación. Tenemos estudiantes que se presentan, están reclutados, y aún pueden recibir unos fondos, sin tener en cuenta su estatus documental. Ellos—los trabajadores intervienen en eso también.

Y en términos de, ¿es replicable esta idea, es un modelo? Parece que otra gente piensa que sí, porque hemos recibido llamados desde todo Boston y varias partes de los Estados Unidos, y también desde otros países. Bien interesantemente, a Colombia le interesa mucho lo que hacemos, México, Brasil—nos encuentran en línea o presentamos en una conferencia.

Lo que hemos empezado a hacer es trabajar en consulta con instituciones y escuelas diferentes y unos lugares de trabajo. Y nos reunimos con las personas que limpian el edificio. Típicamente son inmigrantes, y a menudo son de latinoamérica, pero a veces son de todo el mundo. Y empezamos con los trabajadores y allí vamos. Y preguntamos: Si pudieras hacer más inclusivo su lugar de trabajo, ahora mismo, qué sería? Y entonces estamos trabajando en aquello. Y identificamos a personas a cuales les interesan y les importan estos asuntos dentro de la institución. Y trabajamos con los mayores, y los jóvenes, y—porque todos tienen varios tipos de poder en la misma institución.

Y así es cómo empezamos con ellos a crear cambios. Y lo hacemos a través de simplemente sentarnos alrededor de la mesa y hablar. Hacemos proyectos de escritura juntos, contamos las historias, hacemos comida, y es un proceso muy lento y meticuloso por que se trata de la confianza, y se trata de riesgo: Las personas cuyas historias tienen más que a todas para enseñarnos arriesgan más que a nadie para decirnos, y típicamente se benefician menos que a nadie. Así que tenemos que trabajar en construirnos confianza, y tiene que ser auténtico, porque si no, todo se deshace en cinco segundos.

Baez: Sí, creo que es muy interesante lo que dijiste sobre—simplemente empieza con sentarse alrededor de una mesa de conferencia, y sólo hablar, y conocerse, y confianza. Y me siento que esto es algo que le falta mucho al mundo en general—simplemente entendiendo a la persona que está sentado frente de ti. Puede que ustedes hubieran venido de circunstancias muy diferentes, y simplemente entenderles es el primer paso.

Gjergji: Y el primer paso también podría ser el decir “Hola” a las personas que trabajan en nuestros entornos y crear una comunicación y discurso acerca de la identidad y nuestras experiencias. Es un gran proyecto. ¿Nos pueden decir un poco acerca de Mobility Movilidad, y cómo cabe Proyecto Carrito?

Catalani: Claro, Mobility Movilidad es una organización sin ánimo de lucro que Tam y yo y otro director, Jota Samper, empezamos el año pasado. Y surgió del trabajo que hemos hecho juntos por los últimos cuatro años. Proyecto Carrito es uno de nuestros tres iniciativas primarias que tenemos. Y todos se tratan de esta idea de que Tam estaba hablando, de unir a personas que típicamente no se unen, y coreografiar conversaciones con audiencias diversas acerca de historias que muchas veces no se escuchan.

Entonces los otros proyectos que tenemos: Uno es un archivo de documentales en Medellín, Colombia, donde trabajamos con mujeres y familias que fueron forzados a huir de sus hogares, usualmente en las áreas rurales, debido a violencia o pobreza, y a reconstruir sus hogares en la ciudad de Medellín—y han hecho esto por los últimos 50 años. Entonces este archivo es un registro histórico de cómo la ciudad de Medellín realmente fue construida, por las personas que realmente la construyeron, con sus propias manos. Y lo hacemos cada verano—trabajamos con más contadores de historias para crear más documentales.

Y otro—el otro proyecto que tenemos es que trabajamos con artistas colombianos jóvenes y emergentes para ayudarles a venir y viajar con su arte—a cruzar fronteras con su arte y sus historias. Les ayudamos a venir a los EEUU para exponer su arte, y también hablar con audiencias acerca de sus obras, las cuales típicamente tratan del siglo 21—de unos de nuestros asuntos más urgentes de sostenibilidad y derechos humanos. Y hablan acerca de estos asuntos bien globales a través de perspectivas bien locales. Esto es muy poderoso para audiencias a lo largo del país que ven sus obras y les escuchan hablar acerca de ellas.

Marko: Trabajan en derechos de agua, derechos de mujer, cultura de jóvenes, arte hip hop, diferentes cosas que se producen. Y frecuentemente no sólo tratan de crisis humanitarias y ambientales, sino soluciones para ellas. Y a menudo son jóvenes y mujeres, pero no siempre. Y trabajamos con personas de cualquier edad y de cualquier barrio y cualquier formación educativa. Y lo que hacemos es, trabajamos con ellos para entender lo que quieren decir, a quiénes se lo quieren decir, por qué. Y después trabajamos con ellos para hacer todas las formas necesarias de escritura multimedia para que los contadores y las historias crucen fronteras juntos. Esto es el parte más difícil, especialmente viniendo de un lugar como Colombia—conseguir un visado y demás.

Coreografiamos toda esta comunicación transnacional con ellos personalmente, en línea. Les preparáremos a ir a la embajada estadounidense, todo tipo de cosas así, para pedir su visado. Luego coreografiamos conversaciones acá en los Estados Unidos. Nuestro última gira, por ejemplo, con nueve artistas jóvenes y emergentes de la Universidad Nacional de Colombia en Medellín, una universidad allá, universidad pública, coreografiamos conversaciones con ellos para que puedan hablar con audiencias en cinco diferentes ciudades en Hawaii, Los Ángeles, Boston, Durham, y Medellín, en Colombia. Y en cada lugar, con cada de aquellas audiencias, es una manera diferente de presentar información.

Es que también—pedimos que la audiencia también haga algún trabajo, también. Esperamos que haya un poco de desafío para las audiencias. No para desafíarles a propósito—pero para que vengan para tener una conversación real. Es un poco diferente de sólo venir al museo y observar algo bonito, que también es importante—creo que tiene su lugar—pero lo que hacemos es un poco diferente. Tratamos la hora que tenemos juntos—o los cinco horas, lo que sea—con nuestras audiencias como momentos sagrados que hacen posible conciencia, cambio social, y la creación de relaciones.

Y lo que intentamos hacer con Mobility Movilidad es que les ayudamos a contadores a crear sus historias en una manera—como hacer un documental, quizás es un ensayo fotográfico, hacen un discurso, también les ayudamos a trabajar con los medios, todas cosas—y después nos retrocedemos. Ryan y yo y Jota, los directores de la organización, nos retrocedemos y después dejamos que las personas que necesitan unirse y tener conversaciones para acabar con la contaminación en un pozo, o hablar acerca de las maneras en que la gente puede tener acceso a la educación—posible. Y nos retrocedemos. Y después las dos personas, o 10 personas, o las 100 personas, que necesitan unirse alrededor de la mesa, siguen uniéndose, y pueden lograr lo que quieren lograr.

Baez: Ya que han mencionado sus otros proyectos, y el impacto que tienen con las personas que visitan en las comunidades—sólo un poco más acerca de la clase de recepción e impacto que piensan que las caravanas, y también incluyendo esto nuevo—estos otros proyectos, han tenido en las comunidades que han visitado. ¿Qué clase de impacto piensan que han tenido con otras personas que—que no saben de ustedes, pero entonces ustedes llegan y les hablan, y expresan esto, qué creen que ellos están pensando?

Marko: Esa es una buena pregunta, y es algo—me obsesiona esto, porque quiero asegurar que lo que estamos haciendo—que les importa a la gente en maneras que son significativas para ellos.

Estuvimos en la Universidad de California, sede Irvine hace unos meses. No se si escucharon—recuerdan esto, pero había un gran logro en todo el sistema de la Universidad de California, de sindicalización de los trabajadores de mantenimiento. Y U.C. Irvine era el sitio de uno de las contenciones más profundas de todo el sistema de la U.C., simplemente porque muchos trabajadores trabajaban bajo presión. No tenían mucho tiempo de vacaciones, y demás. Estaban trabajando en condiciones que eran muy, muy difíciles, y han estado haciéndolo por más de 20 años. Muchos de ellos crían a hijos, por ejemplo, sin seguro de salud, nunca, cosas así.

Fuimos a U.C. Irvine a la invitación de un profesor que quería empezar un proyecto de escritura, nos encontró en línea, nos invitó a venir. Volamos a California desde Boston, y nos reunimos con ellos. Y nos reunimos con como seis—la mayoría mujeres—que eran trabajadores de mantenimiento. Y ya habían tenido grandes logros—habían organizado, se habían sindicalizado, ganaron más dinero, tienen vacaciones—cosas realmente grandes, y también habían recibido mucha atención de los medios, y muchas de ellos ya eran abuelas. Y ellos simplemente hubieran podido decir, “Ya terminamos. Hemos logrado nuestra cosa, ya terminamos.”

Les mostramos nuestro video, lo cual se puede encontrar en nuestro sitio, mobilitymovilidad.org, y les mostramos el mismo video de los trabajadores de aseo acá en Emerson, hablando acerca de lo que hacemos, presentando en una conferencia nacional de estudios composicionales, hablando con profesores de pregrado y administradores. Y las mujeres se quedaban paralizadas, porque dijeron, “Nos sindicalizamos, hemos hecho todas esas cosas, pero todavía somos fantasmas en el campus—no cambiaron mucho nuestras interacciones cotidianas con la gente, en términos de la manera en que experitamos nuestra vida cotidiana en el trabajo. Necesitamos que esto cambie. Eso es lo próximo que tenemos que hacer. Y la única manera en que eso puede pasar es si nos reunimos cara a cara, y nos conocemos.” Y eso es lo que querían hacer.

Y el otro elemento de eso es porque Proyecto Carrito para nosotros es en realidad un carro. “Carrito,” como saben, significa “carro pequeño.” También significa “mi carro querido” por el “-ito,” que es el diminutivo en español. Y precisamente lo que hacemos es publicar nuestro trabajo en un carro, techo a llantas, nuestras historias. Hay fotos gigantes de nosotros, y conducimos, cuando nos vamos a lugares para presentar, lo conducimos y estamos dentro, y después nos bajamos. Estamos en un estacionamiento, así que la gente ve nuestras historias en al lado del carro, o atrás, o en el maletero, o lo que sea, y luego ve que nos bajamos. Y hay fotos de nosotros en el carro, y después nos bajamos del carro, y estamos de pie al lado del carro. Entonces tenemos muchas conversaciones con personas en estacionamientos, en cabinas de peaje, afuera de restaurantes, además de las las presentaciones más formales que hacemos en conferencias. Y finalmente este verano, vamos a hacer una caravana, y Ryan puede hablar más acerca de esto.

Catalani: Básicamente queremos hacer más Proyecto Carritos con otras instituciones, u otros grupos que les interesa este trabajo de hacer más inclusivo, global, y humano nuestro sistema educativo, y deberían ser esfuerzos liderados por inmigrantes también. Y vamos a conducir este verano, en Julio, desde Boston a la frontera San Diego-Tijuana, con otros carros y otros grupos que van a crear carros o minivans u otros vehículos, envolverlos con sus historias, y sus sueños y prácticas para un sistema educativo más inclusivo.

Y conduciremos juntos, a través de una ruta escogida muy cuidadosamente, a lo largo del país, y parar estratégicamente para hablar con audiencias que hemos desarrollado, que hemos preparado para hablar con nosotros. Y terminaremos en la Costa Oeste, y nos reuniremos. Y el viaje probablemente tomará como dos semanas. Y los grupos interesados todavía nos pueden contactar en el sitio, y hablarnos acerca de cómo pueden ser parte de la Caravana este verano.
