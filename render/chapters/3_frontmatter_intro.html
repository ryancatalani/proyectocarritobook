    Introduction

    Tamera Marko, Ph.D.

    Dear Readers,

    Welcome to our book. We are so happy you have come. Thank you for spending some time getting to know us. So much about being an immigrant, especially being an immigrant worker is about being invisible. I am not just talking about the experience of physically crossing international borders. I am also talking about invisible borders in workplaces and schools that institutionalize the mechanisms that keep students, faculty and administrators separated from immigrant workers. These invisible borders, for those of us who have never been stopped at them, are very hard to see and thus to understand the pain of being rejected at them. It might be difficult to even believe that these invisible borders exist, because their very existence contradicts the marrow of a democratic liberal arts education ideal dedicated to diversity and inclusion. Perhaps one way we can all see these borders for a moment is this exercise.

    Imagine your school.

    Do you enter the classroom, the library, the auditorium, the basketball court, or the bathroom as a student, a teacher, an administrator or a janitor? What do you do in these spaces? In simple terms, if you are a student, teacher or administrator, you are expected to come in and have something to say. Be listened to. Do the listening. You are expected to have ideas and questions. Why? So you can learn, eventually applying what you learn to live a better life than you can without a school degree and maybe even, in the rhetoric of many university and college mission statements, make the world a better place.

    Now, imagine you are an immigrant janitor at your school.

    If you are a janitor entering these spaces while students, teachers or administrators are working there, you are expected to be silent. Invisible. You are not expected to come sit down, have ideas, ask questions, write essays, make films, make administrative policy or teach. You are expected to enter the classroom, the library, the auditorium, the basketball court, or the bathroom to do one thing: clean. Why? To clean.

    Then there is the fear. To check out a library book, to sit at a university computer and write an email, to raise your hand and ask a question, to make a comment would be a transgression; it could even possibly cost you your job. Because that is not your job.

    •

    The first year of our writing collective, we made a map of all the school resources that official college policy designates for each staff member. Nearly all the janitors here are Spanish speaking and from Latin America, having had to make the painful and arduous journeys to flee poverty and violence in their home countries to build what they hoped would be a more humane life for themselves and their families in the United States. At our school the janitors are staff. None of the janitors in our collective knew that staff policy extends to college tuition benefits for their children and checking books out of the library. So, we started there. We first invited the directors and staff of human resources and the library to sit with us and meet each other. We learned each other’s names. We looked into each other’s eyes. Then we got to work learning and engaging in putting these policies into practice.

    This is when the invisible borders became visible to us who move freely through spaces in our school in our official and celebrated roles as educators and educated. Because we had formed relationships among each other in our writing collective, seeing one of us suddenly stopped at an invisible border slammed into our hearts. Here is an example.

    On the first day of our writing collective’s library workshop, in which we were going to register for library cards and learn how to check out books, one of the workers in our collective stood paralyzed at the entrance gates that electronically beep if you pass through them without checking out a book at the front desk. Daniel stood perfectly still, beads of sweat on his forehead, his hand gripping the electronic gate. Thinking he was ill, I asked him if he was ok. He said, “Ai, profe, I am not sick. It is just that I cannot do this. The only time I enter this library in all the years I have worked here is to clean it.” Daniel was one of the immigrant janitors in our writing collective who had a college degree from his home country in Latin America. In the past six years of our work together, researching, writing, publishing and giving public lectures and art performances we have been stopped at and negotiated our way through dozens of such invisible borders. At these institutionalized borders, crossing through to the other side always comes down to one element: a person must make a decision to allow the worker to pass through, not just as worker, but also as writer, researcher, teacher. The decision is always two-fold: the worker must decide to make the journey (sign up for a library card, apply for an academic conference, write an essay) and then another person who has the power opens the gate so the immigrant worker can pass through.

    An ongoing example of this is when we request college funds for the workers in our collective to travel to present at an academic conference. Students, professors and administrators all have, as part of college policy, access to apply for plane tickets, lodging and food as part of their professional development. Every time we ask for the same funds for a worker, even when the worker is the main speaker in our panel, we have to ask someone at the college who has the power to make a decision to just decide to say yes. This is because there is no policy at our school that includes academic work, reading and writing, or engaging in the exchange of ideas for workers. We are now in the process of asking to create new policies that allow workers to apply for funding to present at conferences.

    •

    So much about being an immigrant worker is about being invisible. This is why the work we have been doing for the last six years has been about driving our own narratives into plain sight. Furthermore, our process of being and doing, of sitting at the same classroom table together as writers, researchers and teachers we also often do in plain sight. We have learned how powerful it is for students, teachers, administrators and other workers to see us doing what is, both a commonplace and unusual practice. Commonplace because we are just people at school sitting around a table reading, writing, discussing, even arguing about ideas. Unusual, because included at the table are immigrant workers in janitor uniforms, writing, reading and teaching. The presence of immigrant workers, especially in janitor uniform, in our classrooms often startles people as they pass by. We often invite them to come sit awhile with us, and we share our website with them. Sometimes they return. Occasionally, they become part of our collective.

    We have learned it is not just the fact that we work together, it is also the way we are together. When we present in classrooms, in auditoriums, or on the road, people tell us the love and respect we have for each other is so present, palpable. Over the last six years, we have become not just co-writers and researchers, we are friends, familia. We know each other’s families. We have become what in Spanish is called dolientes. It means when she hurts, I too, hurt. To be doliente with someone is the deepest form of respect and trust between friends and colleagues. Time and time again, audience members in our presentations, we find, respond in kind, telling us their own dolor, pain, as immigrants and immigrant workers. They also tell us their joy.

    And this joy, too, we make visible by circulating the stories the immigrant janitors tell us about what they see. An example of this joy and what it can teach us about seeing happened the first time we, at the workers’ request, invited undergraduate students to attend our weekly meeting on campus. When the undergraduates entered the room, the workers each bowed their heads, not meeting the undergraduates’ eyes, even though this was the workers’ writing space and time and the workers were the elders in the room. I later learned this happened because the workers had been instructed in their professional training at our college to be careful about eye contact with students so it would not be misunderstood as a transgression, especially in bathrooms or dormitories. The workers also bowed their heads for another reason. As one worker put it later when we de-briefed this moment so we could more deeply understand each other: “Profe,” Maria said, “it is that the real students entered the room.”

    In that moment, I thought with a pit in my stomach that I as a teacher had failed. Over-reached. Was it too soon to integrate undergraduates and workers here? What could I have done to make this moment work better? Then I realized that there was not much more I could have done.

    This is because we were at the precipice of yet another invisible border, as wide and deep as a ravine: those dividing roles of worker and student. We sat in what was to me a nearly unbearable silence for a few minutes. Finally, another Maria, the elder in the room spoke. It surprised me because she rarely spoke and had never, in a year, spoken first. She said this.

    I have worked here more than 26 years. I attend every graduation. As I watch each of you walk across that stage, I feel so proud, so happy. Because I know I only clean the bathrooms here at this school, but I know my work is a part of your success. I feel so happy in this moment.

    As she spoke, her voice cracked and tears rolled down her face. This did more than break the ice between us. It inspired tears among the undergraduates and the workers, and everyone raised their heads and blinked at each other, holding each other’s gaze. Though we did not know this at the time, this was the beginning of our first tentative steps toward becoming dolientes. This moment, inspired by Maria’s words, bridged the ravine across the invisible student-worker border, and we have been crossing that bridge ever since. That day, in that moment, the codes needed to switch. Maria, not me, had to be the teacher. There would have been no other way. She shifted the power dynamic and, although often invisible herself on campus, spoke with such love of what she herself sees, a seeing that goes beyond her job cleaning and into the heart of the very reason a liberal arts education exists: to graduate students into professionals, into the world.

    •

    I realize some of what I have written could be read as implying judgment, blame. The tone might be read as angry. Mis estimados colegas, that is very far from what I intend. Here, I write this with a dedication to describing the scene and the people and to identify how we maneuver power between us. I hope you might read this introduction as what academics call ethnographic description. This is so we can tell you a little bit about how and why we—janitors, students, faculty and administrators at our small liberal arts college located in one of the most racially and nationality diverse cities in the United States—began working together and why we are still working together six years later. This description of invisibility will help explain why we decided to publish a selection of our work here in this volume. It will also help explain why we decided to publish our ideas and call for educational desegregation on a van and drive our own narrative, in plain view, across the entire country.

    The tone I intend here is the same tone that the immigrant workers have initiated and carefully taught us all how to practice and sustain in everything we do in our writing collective: love and radical patience. This love and patience is our form of cultural expression. It is also strategic resistance to institutionalized inequality and segregation and our embodiment of the desegregation we know is possible because we practice it with each other. Specifically, we work together not as a social welfare project in terms of students and faculty “helping” “poor workers.” Rather, we each come to the same table to together define our questions, problems and investigative and rhetorical approaches as researchers, teachers and writers. This is why after working together for a year, we stopped calling ourselves an English Language class and instead embraced our group for what we had quickly become: a research writing collective, which we named Students for Rhetorical Mobility. In our collective, we are all researchers, teachers and writers.

    This took me time to grasp what we were doing. To be honest, I was (and am) angry about inequality and invisible borders. In the first years of our writing collective, sometimes I could not sleep at night because I fretted over my concern that we were not doing enough. Shouldn’t we be unionizing? Shouldn’t we be protesting? We are just talking, writing, singing and eating together. As a teacher and human rights activist, I struggled with the pace and seemingly benign approach to our work in the beginning.

    However, once we began publishing our work and presenting it to audiences throughout our campus and then throughout the United States and in Mexico, Brazil, Colombia, France and other countries, I realized that there is great power in what we are doing. Once I sat back and really thought about it, I realized that actually the work we have been doing has been one of sustained stamina, longevity and deep research that is rare in any research project. It is not easy to be with each other—with the raging contradictions between us. Every few years, our undergraduate student writers graduate and leave. Most of them stay in steady contact with us. When we travel to present our work, they open their homes to us, cook us dinner. Some former students even attend our weekly meetings via Skype, wherever in the world they are working now. Over the years, at each presentation we give of our work, there is always one audience member who remains in contact with us—for years. Sometimes audience members drive hours to see us present again. Yet, the workers always stay in their same jobs, cleaning.

    •

    I grew up in the San Diego-Tijuana border region in the 1970s and 1980s, living and studying with children and their families who were newly arrived immigrants, fleeing violence and poverty in Vietnam, the Philippines, Mexico and Central America. Later, I worked as a human rights journalist in post-war reconciliation zones in Africa, Asia, Latin America and the United States. All of that informs my work as an academic engaged in multimedia, community-based research and archive-building in conflict and peace zones in Colombia and the Americas. I feel that negotiating crossing borders and the code switching, sometimes step by step (from classroom to lecture hall to community meeting) is part of my very bone marrow. However, I am also aware that my border crossing is mostly a privilege. My border crossing is highly visible and I, as a journalist, storyteller, researcher, archivist, and teacher have been rewarded for this crossing. In fact, this border crossing is my job.

    I have published elsewhere, at length, about two concepts. The first concept I call doble desplazamiento, double displacement. That is, when people first become displaced from their homes and communities and then they become displaced from their own stories of displacement. In our work here, we seek to disrupt doble desplazamiento by presenting our stories in our own words, languages, images and cultural narrative forms.

    The second concept is about identifying and negotiating rhetorical risk. That is the risk that people make in telling their own stories. All too often, those who have the most to teach us about immigration and immigrants have the least access to cross borders to do so. Furthermore, those who risk the most to tell their stories often benefit the least from telling them. This is why we, as a writing collective, ranging in ages from 18 to 75 and each coming from different lived experiences, had to decide, together, to come to the table, look into each other’s eyes and slowly, over time, become dolientes. The collection of writing in this book was only possible because of the trust we developed as writers and human beings over time, trust and respect that grew as we learned to see what before had been invisible to us.

    We seek in Proyecto Carrito and all the work we do in our writing collective to embody the premise that inspires the storytelling nonprofit I co-founded with Ryan Catalani, Jota Samper and Sylvia Spears. I believe we already have everything we need to change the world. It is just a matter of perspective. In our collective this means that we bring people to the table who would not usually come to the table but who have the most to teach us about immigration and how to make our schools more inclusive. We do so, however, in mutual gaze, into the contradictions and inequality among us. The students continue to graduate. The workers continue to clean. We stay together and continue to ask, what do we want to about this?

    •

    This is why we asked immigrant janitors in our writing collective to write about what they would change to make our school more humane by making it more inclusive. As a group, they asked for three things.

    Teach the real reasons why people immigrate to the United States and other countries.

    Be more united among Latinxs across all job titles on campus.

    Say good morning to us.

    Tired of being invisible because they wear a janitor’s uniform, immigrant janitors in our writing collective are mainly asking to be seen. In classroom talks, public performances, conference presentations, an open letter online and documentary videos, they have been asking for the members of our college community to say good morning to them. This request has inspired dozens of greetings between janitors, students, faculty and administrators every day throughout our campus’s hallways, bathrooms, classrooms, dormitories and surrounding sidewalks: good morning, buenos días, ¿cómo estás?, ¿qué has hecho?

    The current turn in U.S. and global political leadership now gives the timing of our work an even more urgent edge. Given that by 2050 an estimated one-sixth of the world’s population will be displaced and live in communities they had to build with their own hands, I believe we live in an era of global crisis that is not just a human rights or environmental crisis. We are living in an era of global rhetorical crisis. That is, who gets to tell their stories and who will listen? If we are to make our schools, our communities, our countries, our borders more inclusive, who will we invite to the table to collaborate on solutions? If we want, for example, to understand immigration, might we invite those who have deep expertise and lived experience with immigration—that is, the immigrant workers in our everyday lives?

    The experience of being part of the Proyecto Carrito Caravan has inspired me with deep hope. Driving across the 3,300 miles across the United States from Boston to San Diego reminded me so much that we are all part of a planet and natural landscape of oceans, mountains, plains, forests and deserts. Mario Ernesto Osorio, an immigrant janitor and main speaker in our Caravan marveled at this too. As we labored to drive up the Rocky Mountains in Colorado, on our way to California, Mario Ernesto looked up at the massive boulders, dusted with snow, and said in a quiet voice filled with awe, “Human beings carved this road through this mountain. Imagine that. That this is possible.”

    I was deeply impacted—stunned and inspired—by what we learned at each place across the country that we presented Proyecto Carrito and our writing collective work. At private universities in Colorado, large public schools in California, art museums in Chicago, and parking lots of small liberal arts colleges, we were met with the same deep interest and enthusiasm among audiences. This, despite the fact that these were widely diverse audiences in each place: professors, students, security guards, parking lot attendants, janitors, Chicano lowrider associations, artists, museum curators, architects, urban planners, poets, mothers, youth. Something about Proyecto Carrito resonated with all of them. They continue to write us, seeking ways we can collaborate. It stunned me to see that at each place, there are people ready to decide to see and open the invisible borders at each of their institutions to make their schools and workplaces more inclusive.

    This is not to diminish the also very real existence of fear, risk and border (re)enforcement. It is also important to say that we drove this Caravan about a month before the U.S. Presidential Election. Even then, we knew, we could feel that our very presence, together with the pro-immigrant worker message we were circulating, inspired fear sometimes. I, as a white, U.S. born, native English speaking college teacher with a Ph.D. and years of teaching experience, shook with fear a few times along the road, knowing that Proyecto Carrito’s pro-immigrant message could diminish my white privilege in the eyes of some. Our van, making our message so visible combined with us—immigrant worker, faculty member and young alumnus—so visibly connected with this message, put us at risk in some public spaces. We had to think carefully about where we might stop for gas or to stay for the night. In this way, when we were inside the van or stepping in and out of it, the message became like a temporary skin. A skin that revealed on which side of an argument we stand.

    Furthermore, we began driving from Boston without knowing where the funds would come to complete the journey. This was because just a few days before we departed, we suddenly had to repair the car engine, significantly depleting our travel funds. Each day, as the miles rolled past the Proyecto Carrito car windows, we clapped and cheered from our driver and passenger seats each time Ryan’s phone beeped, giving us notice we had received another donation so we could keep going. To be honest, in my 20s, this funding-on-the-move kind of thing might have been exhilarating. Twenty years later, I found the process unnerving. Yet, that is what the initial immigration journey felt like for our writing collective workers. Again, the process we negotiate to be able to write and present our writing together teaches me more and more about the pain and joy of negotiating borders.

    However, more than the risk and fear, there has been so much joy. At every presentation we gave, people always commented as much on our published work as they did on the fact that we were doing it together and in such a monumental way over thousands of miles, spending time together 24/7. We learned the impact it had on people simply seeing us work together. This deepened when people knew more about how we accomplished this work, and what we just decided we needed to do. And then did it. Mario Ernesto took his entire year’s vacation from his two jobs as a janitor to come on the Caravan. This means he cannot use that time to return to see his family in El Salvador for a year. He was also away from his beloved five-year-old daughter for two weeks. Ryan, who recently graduated from our college, for the last two years has been raising his own funding to build our nonprofit and has filmed, edited and circulated all of the documentary videos, photographs, blogs and website we have for this and other related projects. Ramiro Soto, another immigrant worker in our collective, donated his family van, which became the first Proyecto Carrito van. I drove our own family car for the second Proyecto Carrito Caravan.

    To our surprise, people at each of our Caravan stops wanted to talk with us—for hours—after we had given our presentation. We were delighted and stayed for as long as people wanted to exchange ideas. This meant, however, that we then had to cram 12–17 hours of driving in a day to arrive at our next presentation on time. Once we drove through the night. I had, before the Caravan, torn my hamstring in two places and so spent that night jostling in the back seat with my throbbing leg elevated and Ryan and Mario Ernesto taking turns driving since sunset after we had worked 10 hours straight. We rolled in, bleary-eyed, to Boulder, Colorado with two hours to spare before giving our next presentation to 100 students. We could not have been more tired, nor more happy. Proyecto Carrito, given its local and now national and even global resonance among people who can make decisions to make their schools more inclusive in small and big ways, is the best work I have done in my life. I am lucky to be part of the Students for Rhetorical Mobility and Proyecto Carrito family. This work makes it possible for me to synthesize all of my life’s work and experience in ways that, concretely, indeed do change the world. At least, our world. This work, I know, is only just beginning.

    Boston, Massachusetts

    January 12, 2017

This description of invisibility will help explain why we decided to publish a selection of our work here in this volume. It will also help explain why we decided to publish our ideas and call for educational desegregation on a van and drive our own narrative, in plain view, across the entire country.

    The tone I intend here is the same tone that the immigrant workers have initiated and carefully taught us all how to practice and sustain in everything we do in our writing collective: love and radical patience. This love and patience is our form of cultural expression. It is also strategic resistance to institutionalized inequality and segregation and our embodiment of the desegregation we know is possible because we practice it with each other. Specifically, we work together not as a social welfare project in terms of students and faculty “helping” “poor workers.” Rather, we each come to the same table to together define our questions, problems and investigative and rhetorical approaches as researchers, teachers and writers. This is why after working together for a year, we stopped calling ourselves an English Language class and instead embraced our group for what we had quickly become: a research writing collective, which we named Students for Rhetorical Mobility. In our collective, we are all researchers, teachers and writers.

    This took me time to grasp what we were doing. To be honest, I was (and am) angry about inequality and invisible borders. In the first years of our writing collective, sometimes I could not sleep at night because I fretted over my concern that we were not doing enough. Shouldn’t we be unionizing? Shouldn’t we be protesting? We are just talking, writing, singing and eating together. As a teacher and human rights activist, I struggled with the pace and seemingly benign approach to our work in the beginning.

    However, once we began publishing our work and presenting it to audiences throughout our campus and then throughout the United States and in Mexico, Brazil, Colombia, France and other countries, I realized that there is great power in what we are doing. Once I sat back and really thought about it, I realized that actually the work we have been doing has been one of sustained stamina, longevity and deep research that is rare in any research project. It is not easy to be with each other—with the raging contradictions between us. Every few years, our undergraduate student writers graduate and leave. Most of them stay in steady contact with us. When we travel to present our work, they open their homes to us, cook us dinner. Some former students even attend our weekly meetings via Skype, wherever in the world they are working now. Over the years, at each presentation we give of our work, there is always one audience member who remains in contact with us—for years. Sometimes audience members drive hours to see us present again. Yet, the workers always stay in their same jobs, cleaning.

    •

    I grew up in the San Diego-Tijuana border region in the 1970s and 1980s, living and studying with children and their families who were newly arrived immigrants, fleeing violence and poverty in Vietnam, the Philippines, Mexico and Central America. Later, I worked as a human rights journalist in post-war reconciliation zones in Africa, Asia, Latin America and the United States. All of that informs my work as an academic engaged in multimedia, community-based research and archive-building in conflict and peace zones in Colombia and the Americas. I feel that negotiating crossing borders and the code switching, sometimes step by step (from classroom to lecture hall to community meeting) is part of my very bone marrow. However, I am also aware that my border crossing is mostly a privilege. My border crossing is highly visible and I, as a journalist, storyteller, researcher, archivist, and teacher have been rewarded for this crossing. In fact, this border crossing is my job.

    I have published elsewhere, at length, about two concepts. The first concept I call doble desplazamiento, double displacement. That is, when people first become displaced from their homes and communities and then they become displaced from their own stories of displacement. In our work here, we seek to disrupt doble desplazamiento by presenting our stories in our own words, languages, images and cultural narrative forms.

    The second concept is about identifying and negotiating rhetorical risk. That is the risk that people make in telling their own stories. All too often, those who have the most to teach us about immigration and immigrants have the least access to cross borders to do so. Furthermore, those who risk the most to tell their stories often benefit the least from telling them. This is why we, as a writing collective, ranging in ages from 18 to 75 and each coming from different lived experiences, had to decide, together, to come to the table, look into each other’s eyes and slowly, over time, become dolientes. The collection of writing in this book was only possible because of the trust we developed as writers and human beings over time, trust and respect that grew as we learned to see what before had been invisible to us.

    We seek in Proyecto Carrito and all the work we do in our writing collective to embody the premise that inspires the storytelling nonprofit I co-founded with Ryan Catalani, Jota Samper and Sylvia Spears. I believe we already have everything we need to change the world. It is just a matter of perspective. In our collective this means that we bring people to the table who would not usually come to the table but who have the most to teach us about immigration and how to make our schools more inclusive. We do so, however, in mutual gaze, into the contradictions and inequality among us. The students continue to graduate. The workers continue to clean. We stay together and continue to ask, what do we want to about this?

    •

    This is why we asked immigrant janitors in our writing collective to write about what they would change to make our school more humane by making it more inclusive. As a group, they asked for three things.

    Teach the real reasons why people immigrate to the United States and other countries.

    Be more united among Latinxs across all job titles on campus.

    Say good morning to us.

    Tired of being invisible because they wear a janitor’s uniform, immigrant janitors in our writing collective are mainly asking to be seen. In classroom talks, public performances, conference presentations, an open letter online and documentary videos, they have been asking for the members of our college community to say good morning to them. This request has inspired dozens of greetings between janitors, students, faculty and administrators every day throughout our campus’s hallways, bathrooms, classrooms, dormitories and surrounding sidewalks: good morning, buenos días, ¿cómo estás?, ¿qué has hecho?

    The current turn in U.S. and global political leadership now gives the timing of our work an even more urgent edge. Given that by 2050 an estimated one-sixth of the world’s population will be displaced and live in communities they had to build with their own hands, I believe we live in an era of global crisis that is not just a human rights or environmental crisis. We are living in an era of global rhetorical crisis. That is, who gets to tell their stories and who will listen? If we are to make our schools, our communities, our countries, our borders more inclusive, who will we invite to the table to collaborate on solutions? If we want, for example, to understand immigration, might we invite those who have deep expertise and lived experience with immigration—that is, the immigrant workers in our everyday lives?

    The experience of being part of the Proyecto Carrito Caravan has inspired me with deep hope. Driving across the 3,300 miles across the United States from Boston to San Diego reminded me so much that we are all part of a planet and natural landscape of oceans, mountains, plains, forests and deserts. Mario Ernesto Osorio, an immigrant janitor and main speaker in our Caravan marveled at this too. As we labored to drive up the Rocky Mountains in Colorado, on our way to California, Mario Ernesto looked up at the massive boulders, dusted with snow, and said in a quiet voice filled with awe, “Human beings carved this road through this mountain. Imagine that. That this is possible.”

    I was deeply impacted—stunned and inspired—by what we learned at each place across the country that we presented Proyecto Carrito and our writing collective work. At private universities in Colorado, large public schools in California, art museums in Chicago, and parking lots of small liberal arts colleges, we were met with the same deep interest and enthusiasm among audiences. This, despite the fact that these were widely diverse audiences in each place: professors, students, security guards, parking lot attendants, janitors, Chicano lowrider associations, artists, museum curators, architects, urban planners, poets, mothers, youth. Something about Proyecto Carrito resonated with all of them. They continue to write us, seeking ways we can collaborate. It stunned me to see that at each place, there are people ready to decide to see and open the invisible borders at each of their institutions to make their schools and workplaces more inclusive.

    This is not to diminish the also very real existence of fear, risk and border (re)enforcement. It is also important to say that we drove this Caravan about a month before the U.S. Presidential Election. Even then, we knew, we could feel that our very presence, together with the pro-immigrant worker message we were circulating, inspired fear sometimes. I, as a white, U.S. born, native English speaking college teacher with a Ph.D. and years of teaching experience, shook with fear a few times along the road, knowing that Proyecto Carrito’s pro-immigrant message could diminish my white privilege in the eyes of some. Our van, making our message so visible combined with us—immigrant worker, faculty member and young alumnus—so visibly connected with this message, put us at risk in some public spaces. We had to think carefully about where we might stop for gas or to stay for the night. In this way, when we were inside the van or stepping in and out of it, the message became like a temporary skin. A skin that revealed on which side of an argument we stand.

    Furthermore, we began driving from Boston without knowing where the funds would come to complete the journey. This was because just a few days before we departed, we suddenly had to repair the car engine, significantly depleting our travel funds. Each day, as the miles rolled past the Proyecto Carrito car windows, we clapped and cheered from our driver and passenger seats each time Ryan’s phone beeped, giving us notice we had received another donation so we could keep going. To be honest, in my 20s, this funding-on-the-move kind of thing might have been exhilarating. Twenty years later, I found the process unnerving. Yet, that is what the initial immigration journey felt like for our writing collective workers. Again, the process we negotiate to be able to write and present our writing together teaches me more and more about the pain and joy of negotiating borders.

    However, more than the risk and fear, there has been so much joy. At every presentation we gave, people always commented as much on our published work as they did on the fact that we were doing it together and in such a monumental way over thousands of miles, spending time together 24/7. We learned the impact it had on people simply seeing us work together. This deepened when people knew more about how we accomplished this work, and what we just decided we needed to do. And then did it. Mario Ernesto took his entire year’s vacation from his two jobs as a janitor to come on the Caravan. This means he cannot use that time to return to see his family in El Salvador for a year. He was also away from his beloved five-year-old daughter for two weeks. Ryan, who recently graduated from our college, for the last two years has been raising his own funding to build our nonprofit and has filmed, edited and circulated all of the documentary videos, photographs, blogs and website we have for this and other related projects. Ramiro Soto, another immigrant worker in our collective, donated his family van, which became the first Proyecto Carrito van. I drove our own family car for the second Proyecto Carrito Caravan.

    To our surprise, people at each of our Caravan stops wanted to talk with us—for hours—after we had given our presentation. We were delighted and stayed for as long as people wanted to exchange ideas. This meant, however, that we then had to cram 12–17 hours of driving in a day to arrive at our next presentation on time. Once we drove through the night. I had, before the Caravan, torn my hamstring in two places and so spent that night jostling in the back seat with my throbbing leg elevated and Ryan and Mario Ernesto taking turns driving since sunset after we had worked 10 hours straight. We rolled in, bleary-eyed, to Boulder, Colorado with two hours to spare before giving our next presentation to 100 students. We could not have been more tired, nor more happy. Proyecto Carrito, given its local and now national and even global resonance among people who can make decisions to make their schools more inclusive in small and big ways, is the best work I have done in my life. I am lucky to be part of the Students for Rhetorical Mobility and Proyecto Carrito family. This work makes it possible for me to synthesize all of my life’s work and experience in ways that, concretely, indeed do change the world. At least, our world. This work, I know, is only just beginning.

    Boston, Massachusetts

    January 12, 2017

    Introducción

    Tamera Marko, Ph.D.

    Estimados lectores,

    Bienvenidos a nuestro libro. Nos encanta que ustedes han venido. Gracias por pasar tiempo conociéndonos. Tanta de la experiencia de ser inmigrante, especialmente de ser trabajador inmigrante, se trata de estar invisible. No solo hablo de la experiencia de físicamente cruzar fronteras internacionales. También hablo de las fronteras invisibles en lugares de trabajo y escuelas que institucionalizan los mecanismos que separan a los estudiantes, profesores, y administradores de los trabajadores inmigrantes. Estas fronteras invisibles, para los de nosotros que nunca hemos estado detenidos por ellas, son muy difíciles ver; así es difícil entender el dolor de ser rechazado por ellas. Puede ser difícil creer que aún existen estas fronteras invisibles, porque su misma existencia contradice el meollo de una ideal de educación democrática de las artes liberales que es dedicada a la diversidad y la inclusión. Quizás alguna manera en que todos podemos ver estas fronteras por un momento es este ejercicio.

    Imagínese su escuela.

    ¿Entra usted al aula, la biblioteca, el auditorio, la cancha de baloncesto, o el baño como estudiante, profesor, administrador, o trabajador de aseo? ¿Qué hace en estos espacios? En términos simples, si es estudiante, profesor, o administrador, se le espera venir y tener algo para decir. Ser escuchado. Escuchar también. Se le espera tener ideas y preguntas. ¿Por qué? Para que pueda aprender, eventualmente aplicando lo que aprende para vivir una vida mejor que pudiera sin un título académico y tal vez aún, en la retórica de las declaraciones de propósitos de muchas universidades, para hacer al mundo un lugar mejor.

    Ahora, imagínese que es un trabajador de aseo en su escuela.

    Si es un trabajador de aseo entrando estos espacios mientras los estudiantes, profesores, o administradores están trabajando allí, se le espera quedar callado. Invisible. No se le espera venir y sentarse, tener ideas, hacer preguntas, escribir ensayos, hacer películas, hacer políticas académicas, o enseñar. Se le espera entrar al aula, la biblioteca, el auditorio, la cancha de baloncesto, o el baño para hacer una cosa: limpiar. ¿Por qué? Para limpiar.

    Además hay el miedo. Sacar un libro de la biblioteca, sentarse en un computador universitario y escribir un correo electrónico, levantarse la mano y hacer una pregunta, hacer un comentario sería una transgresión; puede que le costaría su empleo. Porque no es su trabajo.

    •

    El primer año de nuestro colectivo de escritura, hicimos un mapa de todos de los recursos escolares que la política oficial de la universidad nombra para cada miembro del personal. Casi todos de los trabajadores acá son hispanohablantes y de América Latina. Tenían que tomar la travesía dolorosa y ardua para huir de pobreza y violencia en sus países natales para construir lo que esperaron sería una vida más humana en los Estados Unidos para ellos mismos y sus familias. En nuestra escuela, los trabajadores de aseo son empleados directos de la institución. Ningún de los trabajadores de aseo en nuestro colectivo sabía que la política para empleados incluye beneficios de matrícula universitaria para sus hijos y el derecho de sacar libros de la biblioteca. Entonces empezamos allí. Primero los invitamos a los directores y empleados de recursos humanos y la biblioteca que se sintieran con nosotros y nos conociéramos. Nos aprendemos los nombres. Nos miramos ojo a ojo. Luego empezamos a aprender y involucrarnos en poner estas políticas en práctica.

    Fue en ese momento que nos visibilizó las fronteras invisibles a nosotros, los que movemos libremente a través de espacios en nuestra escuela en nuestros papeles oficiales y celebrados como educadores y educados. Porque habíamos formado relaciones entre nosotros en nuestro colectivo de escritura, el ver uno de nosotros detenido de repente por una frontera invisible nos golpeó nuestras corazones. Aquí está un ejemplo.

    El primer día del taller de nuestro colectivo de escritura con la biblioteca, en el cual íbamos a inscribirnos para tarjetas de biblioteca y aprender cómo sacar libros, uno de los trabajadores en nuestro colectivo quedó paralizado en los portones de entrada que suenan electrónicamente si uno los pasa con un libro y no ha sido aprobado por la recepción. Daniel estaba parado, sin ningún movimiento, su mano agarrando el portón electrónico. Pensándolo enfermo, le pregunté si estaba bien. Dijo, “Ai, profe, no estoy enfermo. Solo es que no puedo hacer esto. La única vez que entro esta biblioteca en todos los años que he trabajado aquí es para limpiarla.” Daniel era uno de los trabajadores de aseo en nuestra colectivo de escritura que tenía un título universitario de su país natal en América Latina. En los últimos seis años de nuestro trabajo juntos, investigando, escribiendo, publicando, y haciendo conferencias públicas y performances artísticas, nos hemos estado detenidos por, y hemos negociado un camino a través de, docenas de tales fronteras invisibles. En estas fronteras institucionalizadas, cruzar al otro lado siempre depende, en última instancia, en un elemento: una persona debe hacer una decisión para permitir que el trabajador pase, no solo como trabajador, pero también con escritor, investigador, profesor. La decisión siempre tiene dos aspectos: el trabajador debe decidir tomar la travesía (registrarse para una tarjeta de biblioteca, presentarse a una conferencia académica, escribir un ensayo) y luego otra persona, que tiene el poder, abre la puerta para que el trabajador inmigrante pueda pasar.

    Un ejemplo en curso de esto es cuando solicitamos fondos universitarios para que los trabajadores de nuestro colectivo puedan viajar para presentar en una conferencia académica. Todos los estudiantes, profesores, y administradores tienen, como parte de la política universitaria, acceso para solicitar pasajes de avión, alojamiento, y comida como parte de su desarrollo profesional. Cada vez que queremos los mismos fondos para un trabajador, aún cuando el trabajador es el orador principal de nuestro panel, tenemos que pedirle a alguien en la universidad que tiene el poder de tomar una decisión para simplemente decir “sí.” Eso es porque no hay ninguna política en nuestra escuela que incluye, para los trabajadores, el trabajo académico, leer y escribir, o involucrarse en el intercambio de ideas. Ahora estamos en el proceso de pedir crear nuevas políticas que permitirían que los trabajadores soliciten fondos para presentar en conferencias.

    •

    Tanta de la experiencia de ser un trabajador inmigrante trata de estar invisible. Eso es porque el trabajo que hemos hecho por los últimos seis años ha tratado de conducir nuestras propias narrativas a plena vista. Además, muchas veces también hacemos a plena vista nuestro proceso de ser y hacer, de sentarnos juntos alrededor de la misma mesa en el aula como escritores, investigadores, y profesores. Hemos aprendido como tan poderoso que es para estudiantes, profesores, administradores, y otros trabajadores vernos hacer lo que es una práctica ambos común e inusual. Común porque solo somos personas en una escuela sentados alrededor de una mesa leyendo, escribiendo, hablando, aún peleando acerca de ideas. Inusual, porque incluidos en la mesa son trabajadores inmigrantes, en sus uniformes de limpieza, escribiendo, leyendo, y enseñando. La presencia de los trabajadores inmigrantes, especialmente en sus uniformes de limpieza, en nuestras aulas a menudo asusta la gente cuando nos pasa. A menudo los invitamos a venir y sentarse un rato con nosotros, y compartimos nuestro sitio web con ellos. A veces vuelven. De vez en cuando, se vuelven parte de nuestro colectivo.

    Hemos aprendido que no solo es el hecho de que trabajamos juntos, sino que también es la manera en que estamos juntos. Cuando presentamos en aulas, en auditorios, o en el camino, la gente nos dice que el amor y respeto que tenemos para todos de nosotros está tan presente, palpable. Durante los últimos seis años, no solo nos hemos vuelto co-escritores e investigadores, sino somos amigos, familia. Conocemos a nuestras familias. Nos hemos vuelto lo que en español se llama dolientes. Significa que cuando ella está dolida, yo también estoy dolida. Ser doliente con alguien es la forma más profunda de respeto y confianza entre amigos y colegas. Una y otra vez, encontramos que miembros de la audiencia de nuestras presentaciones responden de manera similar, diciéndonos de su propio dolor como inmigrantes y trabajadores inmigrantes. También nos cuentan sus alegrías.

    Y esta alegría también visualizamos por circular las historias que los trabajadores de aseo inmigrantes nos cuentan acerca de lo que ven. Un ejemplo de esta alegría y lo que nos puede enseñar acerca de ver ocurrió la primera vez que nosotros, a petición de los trabajadores, los invitamos a estudiantes de pregrado a asistir a nuestra reunión semanal en campus. Cuando los estudiantes de pregrado entraron al cuarto, cada de los trabajadores se inclinó la cabeza y evitaron mirar a los estudiantes de pregrado a los ojos, aunque esto era el espacio y tiempo de escritura de los trabajadores y los trabajadores eran los mayores del cuarto. Luego aprendí que esto pasó porque los trabajadores habían sido ordenados en su capacitación profesional en nuestra universidad a tener cuidado con el contacto visual con estudiantes, para que no fuera malentendido como transgresión, especialmente en los baños o los dormitorios. Los trabajadores también se inclinaron las cabezas por otra razón. Como un trabajador dijo luego cuando hablamos de este momento para poder entendernos más profundamente: “Profe,” dijo Maria, “es que los estudiantes verdaderos entraron al aula.”

    En este momento, pensé con ansiedad que yo como profesora había fracasado. Había intentado hacer demasiado. ¿Era demasiado pronto integrar aquí a estudiantes de pregrado y trabajadores? ¿Qué podría hacer para que este momento funcionara mejor? Entonces me dió cuenta de que no había mucho más que podría haber hecho.

    Eso es porque estábamos en el precipicio de aún otra frontera invisible, una tan amplia y profunda como un barranco: estos papeles divididos de trabajador y estudiante. Nos sentábamos en lo que era, para mí, un silencio casi insoportable por unos minutos. Finalmente, habló otra María, la mayor del cuarto. Me sorprendió porque ella raramente habló y en un año nunca había hablado primero. Dijo esto.

    He trabajado aquí por más de 26 años. Asisto a cada graduación. Mientras los miro a cada de ustedes caminar cruzando este escenario, me siento tan orgulloso, tan feliz. Porque sé que solo limpio los baños aquí en esta escuela, pero sé que mi trabajo es parte de su éxito. Me siento tan feliz en ese momento.

    Mientras hablaba, su voz se quebró y las lágrimas le corrían por su cara. Esto hizo más que romper el hielo entre nosotros. Inspiró lágrimas entre los estudiantes de pregrado y los trabajadores, y todos se levantaron las cabezas y nos pestañeamos, mirándonos fijamente. Aunque no lo sabíamos en el momento, esto era el comienzo de los primeros pasos tentativos hacia volvernos a ser dolientes. Este momento, inspirado por las palabras de María, construyó un puente sobre el barranco, sobre la frontera invisible entre estudiantes y trabajadores, y hemos estado cruzando este puente desde entonces. Ese día, en ese momento, los códigos necesitaron cambiar. María, no yo, tenía que ser la profesora. No hubiera otra manera. Ella cambió el dinámico de poder y, aunque ella misma a menudo sea invisible en el campus, habló con tanto amor de lo que ella misma ve, una mirada que extiende más allá de su trabajo de limpieza y entra al corazón de la misma razón que existe una educación de las artes liberales: para graduarse estudiantes en profesionales, entrando el mundo.

    •

    Comprendo que parte de que lo que he escrito podría ser interpretado como insinuando fallo, culpa. Puede que el tono sea interpretado como enojado. Mis estimados colegas, eso está muy lejos de mi intención. Aquí, escribo esto con una dedicación a describir la escena y las personas y para identificar cómo manejamos el poder entre nosotros. Espero que usted lea esta introducción como lo que académicos llaman una descripción etnográfica. Es para poder contarle un poco de cómo y por qué nosotros—trabajadores de aseo, estudiantes, profesores y administradores en nuestra pequeña universidad de artes liberales, ubicada en una de las ciudades más diversas racialmente y étnicamente de los Estados Unidos—empezamos a trabajar juntos y por qué todavía estamos trabajando juntos seis años luego. Esta descripción de invisibilidad ayudará a explicar por qué decidimos publicar una selección de nuestro trabajo aquí en este tomo. También ayudará a explicar por qué decidimos publicar nuestras ideas y una llamada para la desegregación educativa en una minivan y conducir nuestra propia narrativa, a plena vista, cruzando el país entero.

    Mi intención con el tono aquí es expresar el mismo tono que los trabajadores inmigrantes han iniciado y cuidadosamente nos han enseñado cómo practicar y sostener en todo lo que hacemos en nuestro colectivo de escritura: amor y paciencia radical. Este amor y paciencia es nuestra forma de expresión cultural. También es una resistencia estratégica a la desigualdad y la segregación institucionalizada y nuestra encarnación de la desegregación que sabemos que es posible porque la practicamos entre nosotros. Específicamente, trabajamos juntos no como un proyecto de bienestar social en términos de estudiantes y profesores “ayudando” a “trabajadores pobres.” En realidad, cada de nosotros viene a la misma mesa, y juntos definimos nuestras preguntas, problemas, y enfoques investigativos y retóricos como investigadores, profesores, y escritores. Eso es porque después de trabajar juntos por un año, ya no nos llamamos una clase de aprendizaje de inglés. En vez de esto, nos abrazamos nuestro grupo por lo que rápidamente se volvió a ser: un colectivo de escritura para investigación, la que llamamos Estudiantes por la movilidad retórica. En nuestro colectivo, todos somos investigadores, profesores, y escritores.

    Me tomó tiempo captar lo que hacíamos. Siendo honesta, era (y estoy) enojada por la desigualdad y las fronteras invisibles. En los primeros años de nuestro colectivo de escritura, a veces no pude dormir por noche porque me inquietaba por mi preocupación que no hacíamos suficiente. ¿No nos deberíamos sindicalizar? ¿No deberíamos protestar? Solo estamos hablando, escribiendo, cantando, y comiendo juntos. Como profesora y activista por los derechos humanos, luchaba en el principio con el ritmo y el enfoque aparentemente benigno a nuestro trabajo.

    Sin embargo, en cuanto empezamos a publicar nuestro trabajo y presentarlo a audiencias en todo el campus y luego en todo los Estados Unidos y en México, Brasil, Colombia, Francia, y otros países, me dió cuenta de que hay un gran poder en lo que estamos haciendo. Una vez que me ponía cómoda y realmente lo pensó, me dió cuenta de que el trabajo que hemos estado haciendo ha sido uno de resistencia sostenida, longevidad, e investigación profunda que son raros en cualquier proyecto de investigación. No es fácil estar presente para nosotros—considerando las contradicciones fuertes entre nosotros. Cada pocos años, nuestros estudiantes de pregrado escritores se gradúan y se van. La mayoría de ellos se quedan en contacto fijo con nosotros. Cuando viajamos para presentar nuestro trabajo, nos abren sus casas, nos cocinan la cena. Algunos de los estudiantes antiguos aún asisten nuestras reuniones semanales por Skype, dondequiera en el mundo que ahora estén trabajando. Durante los años, en cada presentación que hacemos de nuestro trabajo, siempre hay un miembro de la audiencia que permanece en contacto con nosotros—por años. A veces los miembros de la audiencia conducen por horas para vernos presentar otra vez. A pesar de todo esto, los trabajadores siempre se quedan en los mismos trabajos, limpiando.

    •

    Crecí en la región fronteriza entre San Diego y Tijuana en los 1970s y 1980s, viviendo y estudiando con niños y sus familias, quienes eran inmigrantes recién llegados, al haber huido violencia y pobreza en Vietnam, las Filipinas, México, y América Central. Luego, trabajaba como periodista de derechos humanos en zonas de reconciliación después del conflicto en África, Asia, América Latina, y los Estados Unidos. Todo esto informa mi trabajo como académica involucrada en investigaciones multimediales y basadas en comunidades, y en la construcción de archivos en zonas de conflicto y paz en Colombia y las Américas. Me siento que negociar el cruzar de fronteras y la alternancia de código, a veces paso por paso (desde el aula al auditorio a la reunión comunitaria) es parte de mi mismo médula ósea. Sin embargo, estoy consciente que mi capacidad de cruzar fronteras es en gran parte un privilegio. Mis cruces de frontera son muy visibles y yo, como periodista, contadora de historias, investigadora, archivera, y profesora he sido premiado por estos cruces. De hecho, esta acción de cruzar es mi trabajo.

    He publicado extensamente en otras partes acerca de dos conceptos. Llamo el primer concepto “doble desplazamiento.” Es decir, cuando personas primero están desplazadas físicamente de sus hogares y comunidades, y segundo están desplazadas de sus propias historias de desplazamiento. En nuestro trabajo aquí, buscamos perturbar el doble desplazamiento a través de presentar nuestras historias en nuestras propias palabras, idiomas, imágenes, y formas narrativas culturales.

    El segundo concepto trata de identificar y negociar el riesgo retórico. Eso es el riesgo que la gente toma cuando cuentan sus propias historias. Con demasiada frecuencia, los que tienen mucho que enseñarnos acerca de la inmigración e inmigrantes tienen el menor acceso a cruzar fronteras para hacerlo. Además, los que arriesgan más para contar sus historias a menudo se benefician menos de contarlas. Esto es por qué nosotros, como colectivo de escritura, de edades comprendidas entre los 18 y los 75 años, cada de nosotros viniendo de una diferente experiencia vivida, tuvimos que decidir, juntos, a venir a la mesa, mirarnos a los ojos, y lentamente, con el tiempo, volvernos a ser dolientes. La colección de escritura en este libro solo era posible por la confianza que hemos desarrollado como escritores y seres humanos con el tiempo, confianza y respeto que crecían mientras aprendíamos ver lo que antes había sido invisible para nosotros.

    Buscamos con Proyecto Carrito, y con todo el trabajo que hacemos en nuestro colectivo de escritura, encarnar la premisa que inspira la organización sin ánimo de lucro dedicada a contar historias que co-fundé con Ryan Catalani, Jota Samper, y Sylvia Spears. Creo que ya tenemos todo lo que necesitamos para cambiar al mundo. Solo es una cuestión de perspectiva. En nuestro colectivo esto significa que los invitamos a la mesa a las personas que típicamente no vendrían a la mesa, pero que tienen mucho para enseñarnos acerca de inmigración y cómo hacer más inclusivas nuestras escuelas. Lo hacemos, sin embargo, en una mirada mutua, entrando en las contradicciones y la desigualdad entre nosotros. Los estudiantes siguen graduándose. Los trabajadores siguen limpiando. Nosotros seguimos juntos y seguimos preguntado, ¿qué queremos hacer acerca de esto?

    Esto es por qué una de nuestras primeras solicitudes como colectivo de escritura de inmigrantes era lo siguiente. Pedimos a los trabajadores de aseo inmigrantes en nuestro colectivo de escritura que escribieran acerca de lo que cambiarían para hacer más humana nuestra escuela a través de hacerla más inclusiva. Como grupo, pidieron tres cosas.

    Enseñar las razones verdaderas de por qué las personas migran a los Estados Unidos y a otros países.

    Ser más unidos entre Latinxs, no importa su papel, en el campus.

    Decirnos buenos días.

    Cansados de estar invisible porque llevan el uniforme de limpieza, los trabajadores de aseo inmigrantes en nuestro colectivo de escritura principalmente están pidiendo que los veamos. En charlas, performances públicas, presentaciones en conferencias, una carta abierta en línea, y videos documentales, han seguido pidiendo que los miembros de nuestra comunidad universitaria les digan buenos días. Esta solicitud ha inspirado docenas de saludos entre trabajadores de aseo, estudiantes, profesores, y administradores cada día en todos los corredores, baños, aulas, dormitorios, y aceras alrededores: buenos días, ¿cómo estás?, ¿qué has hecho?

    El giro actual en liderazgo político estadounidense y mundial ahora imbuye la hora de nuestro trabajo de un significado aún más urgente. Dado que por el 2050 un estimado sexto del población mundial estará desplazado y vivirá en comunidades que tuvieron que construir con sus propias manos, creo que vivimos en una era de crisis global que no solo es una crisis de derechos humanos o del medio ambiente. Estamos viviendo en una crisis global de retórica. Es decir, ¿quién puede contar sus historias, y quienes escucharán? Si vamos a hacer más inclusivas nuestras escuelas, nuestras comunidades, nuestros países, nuestras fronteras, ¿a quienes invitaremos a la mesa para colaborar en soluciones? Si queremos, por ejemplo, entender la inmigración, puede que invitaramos los que tienen pericia profunda y experiencia vivida con inmigración—es decir, los trabajadores inmigrantes en nuestras vidas cotidianas?

    La experiencia de ser parte del Proyecto Carrito Caravana me ha inspirado con una esperanza profunda. Conducir los 3,300 millas (5,300 km) cruzando los Estados Unidos desde Boston a San Diego me recordó tanto que todos somos parte de una planeta y un paisaje natural de océanos, montañas, llanuras, bosques, y desiertos. Mario Ernesto Osorio, trabajador de aseo inmigrante y el orador principal de nuestra Caravana se maravillaba de esto también. Mientras trabajabamos a escalar las Montañas Rocky en Colorado, en ruta a California, Mario Ernesto miró a las rocas enormes, espolvoreadas con nieve, y en una voz baja, llena de asombro, dijo, “Seres humanos tallaron esta calle a través de esta montaña. Imaginate. Que esto es posible.”

    Quedé afectada profundamente—atónita e inspirada—por lo que aprendimos en cada lugar en todo el país en que presentamos Proyecto Carrito y el trabajo de nuestro colectivo de escritura. En universitarias privadas de Colorado, escuelas públicas grandes de California, museos de arte de Chicago, y estacionamientos de universidades pequeñas dedicadas a las artes liberales, los audiencias nos esperaban con el mismo interés profundo y entusiasmo. Esto pasó a pesar de que habían audiencias tan diversas en cada lugar: profesores, estudiantes, vigilantes, empleados de estacionamientos, trabajadores de aseo, asociaciones chicanas de lowriders, artistas, curadores de museos, arquitectos, urbanistas, poetas, madres, jóvenes. Algo acerca de Proyecto Carrito resonó con cada de ellos. Siguen escribiéndonos, buscando maneras en que podemos colaborar. Me dejó antónita ver que en cada lugar, hay personas listas para decidir a ver y abrir las fronteras invisibles en sus respectivas instituciones para hacer más inclusivas sus escuelas y lugares de trabajo.

    Esto no es para disminuir la existencia muy real de miedo, riesgo, y (re)ejecución de la ley fronteriza. También es importante decir que condujimos esta Caravana un mes antes de la elección presidencial estadounidense. Aún así, sabíamos, podríamos sentir que nuestra misma presencia, junto con el mensaje apoyando a los trabajadores inmigrantes que estábamos circulando, a veces inspiraba temor. Yo, como profesora universitaria blanca, nacida estadounidense, nativamente anglohablante, con un doctorado y años de experiencia de enseñar, temblé de miedo unas veces en el camino, sabiendo que el mensaje pro-inmigrante de Proyecto Carrito podría disminuir mi privilegio blanco ante unos. Nuestra minivan, que visibilizaba tanto nuestro mensaje, combinado con nosotros—trabajador inmigrante, profesora, y egresado jóven—tan visiblemente conectados con este mensaje, nos arriesgó en unos espacios públicos. Nos tocaba pensar cuidadosamente acerca de dónde podríamos parar para gasolina o para quedarnos para la noche. En esta manera, cuando estábamos dentro de la minivan, o entrando o saliendo de ella, el mensaje se volvía ser como una piel temporal. Una piel que revelaba en cual lado de un argumento que estamos.

    Además, empezamos a conducir desde Boston sin saber de dónde vendrían los fondos para terminar el viaje. Esto es porque solo unos días antes de salir, de repente tuvimos que reparar el motor del carro, considerablemente reduciendo los fondos para el viaje. Cada día, mientras las calles pasaban las ventanas del carro de Proyecto Carrito, aplaudimos y aclamamos desde nuestros asientos cada vez que sonó el teléfono de Ryan, dándonos noticia que habíamos recibido otra donación para que pudiéramos seguir. Siendo honesta, en mis años 20, puede que este proceso de financiar en el camino fuera excitante. Veinte años luego, encontré desconcertante el proceso. Pero esto es cómo la travesía inicial de inmigración sentía para los trabajadores en nuestro colectivo de escritura. Otra vez, el proceso que negociamos para poder escribir y presentar juntos nuestra escritura me enseña más y más acerca de el dolor y la alegría de negociar fronteras.

    Sin embargo, más que el riesgo y el miedo, ha habido tanta alegría. En cada presentación que hicimos, la gente comentó acerca de nuestro trabajo publicado tanto como del hecho de que lo estábamos haciendo juntos y en una manera tan monumental a lo largo de miles de millas, pasando tiempo juntos 24/7. Aprendimos del impacto en la gente cuando simplemente nos veían trabajar juntos. Esto profundizó cuando la gente se enteró más acerca de cómo llevamos a cabo este trabajo, y lo que simplemente decidimos que necesitamos hacer. Y luego lo hicimos. Mario Ernesto usó todo el tiempo de sus vacaciones anuales de sus dos trabajos de limpieza para venir a la Caravana. Esto significa que no puede usar este tiempo para volver a El Salvador para ver a su familia por un año. También estaba fuera de su querida hija de cinco años por dos semanas. Ryan, quien recientemente se graduó de nuestra universidad, por los últimos dos años ha recaudado sus propios fondos para construir nuestra organización y ha filmado, editado, y circulado todos los videos documentales, fotos, blogs, y sitio web que tenemos para este y cada otro proyecto relacionado. Ramiro Soto, otro trabajador inmigrante de nuestro colectivo, donó su minivan familiar, la cual se convirtió a la primera minivan de Proyecto Carrito. Manejé nuestro propio carro familiar para el segundo Proyecto Carrito Caravana.

    Para nuestra sorpresa, la gente en cada de nuestras paradas querían hablar con nosotros—por horas—después de nuestras presentaciones. Esto nos llenaba de alegría, y nos quedábamos mientras que la gente quería intercambiar ideas. Sin embargo, significaba que entonces nos tocaba conducir por 12–17 horas en un sólo día para llegar a tiempo a nuestra presentación siguiente. Una vez condujimos durante toda la noche. Antes de la Caravana, me había desgarrado el ligamento de la corva en dos lugares, y así pasé esta noche incómoda en el asiento trasero, con mi pierna punzante elevada, y Ryan y Mario Ernesto turnándose para conducir desde el atardecer, después de que habíamos trabajado por 10 horas enseguida. Llegamos adormilados a Boulder, Colorado, con dos horas de anticipación antes de la siguiente presentación para 100 estudiantes. No podríamos haber tenido más sueño, ni estar más contentos. Proyecto Carrito, dado su resonancia local, y ahora nacional y aún global, entre la gente que puede tomar decisiones para hacer sus escuelas más inclusivas en maneras pequeñas y grandes, es el trabajo mejor que he hecho en mi vida. Tengo la suerte de ser parte de la familia de Estudiantes por la movilidad retórica y Proyecto Carrito. Este trabajo me hace posible que sintetice todo el trabajo y la experiencia de mi vida en maneras que, concretamente, sí cambian el mundo. Al menos, nuestro mundo. Este trabajo, lo sé, apenas está empezando.

    Boston, Massachusetts

    12 de enero, 2017
